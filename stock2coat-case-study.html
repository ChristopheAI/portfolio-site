From Friend's Problem to Enterprise Opportunity

"Christophe, kun je mij helpen met het ondersteunen van het volgende?" That's how it started. Nicolas, who I've known for 25 years since I was 16, was launching his powder coating business ML Coating in March 2024. He needed simple software to manage his powder inventory—track consumption, add new containers, get an overview with KPIs.

I did the research. Found nothing that fit. The existing software was either massive ERP systems with hundreds of features Nicolas didn't need, or it was too expensive for a small Flemish business just getting started. Everything was overkill for what he actually needed: "Gewoon simpel iets om mijn voorraad te beheren."

Then I had a realization: This was April 2024, and I'd been exploring AI coding tools like Replit. I saw an opportunity to experiment. "Nicolas," I said, "what if I just build it for you?"

What happened next surprised us both. After some trial and error (I had to restart twice, learning how to properly collaborate with AI agents), I delivered a working web application. Nicolas started using it daily. And then something unexpected happened...

Enterprise Validation: Frank, a consultant from Sherwin-Williams with 39 years in the powder coating industry, visited Nicolas. He saw the webapp. His reaction? "Amai, dat is super interessant!" He immediately called his colleague Laurens. Since June 3rd, we've been meeting weekly, designing Stock2coat as a real business solution.

This isn't a portfolio project anymore—it's enterprise-validated software that caught the attention of industry experts. It proves that modern AI development tools can deliver business-grade solutions when guided by real domain expertise.

Technical Challenges & Solutions

Challenge 1: Mastering AI-Assisted Development

Problem: This was my first time using Replit Agent for a full-stack project. I needed to learn how to effectively communicate with the AI, understand its capabilities and limitations, and guide it to build something that actually worked for Nicolas's business needs.

Solution: Spent time exploring and experimenting with Replit Agent's capabilities. It wasn't immediately successful - I had to learn the right way to prompt it, break down requirements, and iterate when the first attempts didn't work. Through trial and collaboration, the AI handled the React state management, API design, and database setup while I focused on understanding the business requirements and guiding the overall direction.

Challenge 2: Understanding the Business Domain

Problem: I knew nothing about powder coating inventory management. Nicolas had specific workflows around weighing containers, tracking powder consumption, and managing different product types that I needed to translate into code.

Solution: Spent significant time with Nicolas understanding his daily routine. Watched him weigh containers, saw his Excel sheets, and learned why certain data points mattered. This domain knowledge was crucial for building something he'd actually use.

Challenge 3: AI-Assisted Development Learning Curve

Problem: While Replit Agent was powerful, I needed to learn how to effectively collaborate with AI - when to trust it, when to question it, and how to debug when things went wrong.

Solution: Developed a workflow: start with clear requirements, break down complex features into smaller pieces, and always test incrementally. Learned to read and understand the generated code rather than just accepting it blindly.

Challenge 4: Scope vs. Time Pressure

Problem: Nicolas needed this working quickly for his business, but I also wanted to learn modern development practices. Balancing "make it work" with "do it right" was challenging.

Solution: Made pragmatic decisions: skipped authentication to focus on core functionality, used proven patterns (REST API, standard CRUD operations), and prioritized features Nicolas actually needed daily over "nice-to-have" additions.

The Real AI Development Journey: Trial, Error & Community

This was my first real AI coding project. Not the polished workflow you might expect, but the messy, authentic learning process that actually works. Here's what really happened:

🤔 Phase 1: The False Starts (Weeks 1-2)

Multiple AI Tools: Learning What Works

I didn't just use Replit. I tried everything: Claude Artifacts, Bolt.new, Lovable, Windsurf, Cursor. Each had different strengths for UI/UX, but I kept hitting walls. The AI would start doing things I never asked for, going in directions I didn't want. I had to restart twice.

  • First attempt: AI went rogue, added features I didn't want
  • Second attempt: Different tool, similar problems with AI control
  • Frustration with debugging errors AI created
  • Realized I needed to learn AI "steering"

Key learning: AI tools are powerful but need proper guidance and prompting strategy

💡 Phase 2: The Reddit Breakthrough

r/replit Community: Learning from Others

After failing twice, I did what any smart developer does: I went to Reddit. The r/replit subreddit became my classroom. I spent time reading posts, understanding how others collaborated with Replit Agent, learning the best practices the community discovered.

  • Studied successful project workflows on r/replit
  • Learned proper prompting techniques from community
  • Understood Replit Agent's strengths and limitations
  • Got practical tips for AI collaboration

Third time's the charm: Armed with community knowledge, attempt #3 finally got traction

🚀 Phase 3: Finding My AI Development Flow

Replit Agent: Finally Working Together

Third attempt with Replit was different. I had the "feel in my fingers" now - I understood how to collaborate with the AI instead of fighting it. The breakthrough was learning to work with Nicolas's domain expertise, the AI's capabilities, and my own project vision:

  • Nicolas provided business requirements and industry knowledge
  • I translated his needs into clear prompts for the AI
  • Replit Agent built the technical foundation quickly
  • Regular feedback loops with Nicolas kept it on track

The magic: Human expertise + AI speed + real user feedback = working software
